Visualizations

The five visualizations below are designed to move from a broad overview of risk factor patterns down to specific patient-level comparisons. Each chart builds on the previous one to tell a connected story about which factors are most associated with pulmonary disease in this dataset.

Visualization 1 — Indicators Most Associated with Pulmonary Disease

How to interact: Hover over any bar to highlight it and see the indicator name, type, and exact correlation value. Unselected bars fade to make the comparison easier to read.

Takeaway

This chart shows which indicators are most strongly associated with pulmonary disease in the dataset. Smoking stands out as the strongest relationship, followed by smoking family history and symptom-related variables such as throat discomfort and breathing issues. In contrast, age and gender show much weaker relationships, suggesting that lifestyle and symptom indicators are more informative than demographic variables in this analysis.

Visualization 2 — Disease Outcomes by Selected Risk Factors

How to interact: Use the dropdown menu to filter by a specific factor. Hover over any bar to see the exact proportion, raw count, and total group size. "Yes" and "No" indicate whether the patient has that risk factor.

Takeaway

This stacked bar chart compares the proportion of patients with and without pulmonary disease across the selected risk factors shown in the chart. In both cases, patients with the risk factor show a noticeably higher disease rate than those who do not. Smoking shows the strongest difference, with a much higher disease rate among smokers than non-smokers, while disease family history also shows a meaningful gap between groups. This reinforces that these risk-related indicators are more informative than demographic variables in this dataset.

Visualization 3 — Symptom Prevalence Among Disease-Positive Patients

How to interact: Hover over any cell to see the symptom name, age group, prevalence rate, and raw patient counts.

Takeaway

This heatmap shows the prevalence of different indicators among disease-positive patients across age groups. Smoking and breathing issues remain the most prevalent across all age groups, while other indicators such as family history and stress-related variables appear at lower rates overall. The chart suggests that many of the strongest indicator patterns remain fairly consistent across age groups, reinforcing that age alone is not the main differentiator in this dataset.

Visualization 4 — Age vs Oxygen Saturation by Disease Status

How to interact: Use the dropdown to filter by smoking status. Hover over any point to see the patient's age, oxygen saturation, disease status, and smoking status.

Takeaway

This scatter plot shows the relationship between age and oxygen saturation for individual patients, colored by disease status. The two groups overlap substantially, but disease-positive patients tend to appear at somewhat lower oxygen saturation levels overall. This suggests that oxygen saturation may be a more useful physiological signal than age alone for distinguishing disease status in this dataset. The dropdown filter also helps show whether this overall pattern remains similar within smoking groups.

Visualization 5 — Patient Profile Comparison: Disease vs. No Disease

How to interact: Hover over any dot on the radar chart to see the exact factor name, group, and prevalence rate.

Takeaway

This radar chart provides a broader comparison of symptom and indicator prevalence between disease-positive and disease-negative patients. The disease-positive group is consistently higher across several dimensions, especially smoking, breathing issues, and throat discomfort. Other indicators such as disease family history and stress-related factors show smaller differences, but still contribute to the overall profile. This chart makes it easier to compare the general shape of the two groups rather than focusing on any single variable alone.

Conclusion

Overall, our analysis suggests that smoking is the strongest indicator associated with pulmonary disease in this dataset, with smoking family history and several symptom-related variables also standing out. Across the visualizations, lifestyle and symptom indicators appear to be more informative than demographic variables such as age and gender, which show much weaker relationships throughout the analysis.

The heatmap supports this by showing that many symptom and indicator patterns remain fairly consistent across age groups, which suggests that age alone is not the strongest differentiator. The scatter plot adds a physiological perspective by showing that disease-positive patients tend to appear at somewhat lower oxygen saturation levels, even though the two groups still overlap considerably.

The radar chart brings these findings together by comparing broader patient profiles side by side. The overall shape difference between disease-positive and disease-negative patients is driven most clearly by smoking, breathing issues, and throat discomfort, while other indicators such as disease family history and stress-related factors show smaller but still visible differences.

Limitations and Future Work

This project uses a synthetic dataset, so the findings should be interpreted as patterns within the data rather than medical proof. In future work, these visualizations could be extended by testing the same patterns on real clinical data, adding more environmental or medical history variables, or building a predictive model to evaluate how well these indicators work together in identifying pulmonary disease risk.
