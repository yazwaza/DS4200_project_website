What Predicts Pulmonary Disease?

DS4200: Information Presentation and Data Visualization

Introduction

Our project examines how health indicators, lifestyle-related factors, and symptoms relate to pulmonary disease risk. Using a synthetic lung cancer risk dataset from Kaggle, we aim to visualize which factors stand out most clearly and how they help describe patterns of risk.

Data Overview

We used the "Lung Cancer Prediction Dataset" from Kaggle, featuring 5,000 patient-level records and 18 attributes[cite: 7, 25, 26]. The final cleaned version contains 5,000 usable observations with a mix of categorical (Smoking, Family History, Symptoms) and continuous (Age, Oxygen Saturation) variables[cite: 27, 30].

Visualizations

Category: Risk Factors

Visualization 1 — Indicators Most Associated with Pulmonary Disease

How to interact: Hover over any bar to highlight it and see exact correlation values.
What to look for: Observe how Smoking and Family History significantly outweigh demographic variables like Age.

Takeaway: Smoking stands out as the strongest relationship in the dataset, while demographic factors show very weak relationships, suggesting lifestyle is a more informative predictor here.

Category: Risk Factors

Visualization 2 — Disease Outcomes by Top Risk Factors

How to interact: Use the dropdown menu to filter by specific factors (Smoking, Family History).
What to look for: Compare the "Yes" vs "No" bars to see the dramatic spike in disease rates for smokers.

Takeaway: Patients with high-risk lifestyle factors show over 5x higher disease rates than those without, confirming these factors as meaningful primary predictors.

Category: Symptoms & Indicators

Visualization 3 — Symptom Prevalence Among Disease-Positive Patients

How to interact: Hover over any heatmap cell to see specific symptom prevalence rates.
What to look for: Notice the consistent high prevalence of breathing issues across all age groups.

Takeaway: Symptom profiles are remarkably consistent across age groups, reinforcing that physical indicators like breathing issues are universal "red flags" regardless of patient age.

Category: Symptoms & Indicators

Visualization 4 — Age vs Oxygen Saturation by Disease Status

How to interact: Use the dropdown to filter by smoking status. Hover over points for patient details.
What to look for: Look for the cluster of disease-positive points at lower Oxygen Saturation levels (typically below 92%).

Takeaway: Disease-positive patients cluster at lower oxygen saturation levels, suggesting this physiological metric is a stronger clinical indicator than age alone.

Category: Broader Patient Profiles

Visualization 5 — Risk Factor Profile: Disease vs. No Disease

How to interact: Hover over dots on the radar chart to see specific factor prevalence in each group.
What to look for: Compare the "shape" of the polygons; the disease-positive group creates a much larger area across all symptom axes.

Takeaway: This holistic view shows that disease-positive patients possess a distinct "profile" dominated by a cluster of symptoms (smoking, breathing issues, throat discomfort) compared to healthy patients.

Conclusion

Key Takeaways

  • Lifestyle is Paramount: Smoking is the single most dominant predictor of pulmonary disease in this dataset, far exceeding the influence of demographics[cite: 131, 138].
  • Universal Symptom Markers: Breathing issues and throat discomfort are consistent indicators across all age groups, serving as reliable clinical markers for risk[cite: 138].
  • Physiological Indicators: Measurable signals like lower oxygen saturation provide a clearer threshold for disease presence than broad patient demographics[cite: 111].

Limitations & Future Work

A primary limitation is the use of a synthetic dataset; future work should validate these interactive models against real clinical data and incorporate environmental factors like long-term pollution exposure[cite: 100, 134].
